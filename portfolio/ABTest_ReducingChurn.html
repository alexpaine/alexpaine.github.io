Customer Churn: Loyalty Rewards and A/B Testing¶

Customer acquisition and retention are key drivers of sustainable growth. Whilst acquisition grows the customer/revenue base, retention increases customer lifetime value (LTV). With a limited marketing budget it is essential to consider which (or what combination of the two) offers the best return on investment (ROI). Naturally, this will depend on a variety of factors relating to the industry and the organisation, including its' strengths, weaknesses, needs and objectives. At the risk of making a sweeping generalisation, retention is often cheaper and yields a greater ROI, particularly for subscription based businesses (i.e. telco's, SaaS businesses) whereas businesses that depend on large, one-time customer purchases may be better served by focusing on acquisition initiatives.

This notebook will focus on the results of a retention campaign carried out by a fictional telecommunications company to demonstrate how A/B testing can be used to evaluate the success of a loyalty reward scheme. The notebook will take a computational approach to A/B testing which uses intuitive and 'formula-light' resampling methods.

The data for the notebook is retrieved from the IBM Accelerator Catalog. Links to the specific datasets used in the notebook can be found in the 'import' section of the notebook.

def merge(left, right):
  left_col = left.columns
  right_col = right.columns
  difference = right_col.difference(left_col)
  merged =  pd.merge(left, right[difference], left_index=True, right_index=True)
  return merged

Permutation Testing - could the observed effect be a result of random chance?¶

Hypothesis Tests help us assess whether random chance might be responsible for the observed effect (i.e. the reduction in churn rate). They start with the baseline assumption that the difference between groups is due to random chance (the null hypothesis). The purpose of the test is to see if the null hypothesis can be rejected by showing that the outcomes for groups A and B are more different than what chance might produce.

Permutation testing provides an intuitive approach to hypothesis testing that involves:

  1. Shuffling results from the groups A and B
  2. Sampling the shuffled results with sample sizes equal to the sizes of groups A and B
  3. Observing how often we get a difference as extreme as the observed difference

If the observed difference lies outside most of the permuted differences then we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the difference is 'statistically significant'.

def permute(df, group, A, B, outcome, iterations, func):
  #initialise list for test statistic replicates
  perm_reps = []
  #slice outcomes
  samples = df[outcome]
  #calculate group size for slicing
  nA = len(df[df[group] == A])
  # iterate calculations
  for i in range(iterations):
    # permute or 'shuffle' outcomes 
    perm = np.random.permutation(samples)
    # slice permuted outcomes to create perm samples for A & B
    perm_A = perm[:nA]
    perm_B = perm[nA:]
    # calc test statistic and append to list
    perm_reps += [func(perm_A) - func(perm_B)]
  return np.array(perm_reps)

Visually, we can see that the majority of the area under the graph lies to the right of the observed difference of means i.e. most of the permuted samples had either reduced reductions in churn rate or increased churn rates.

The p-value provides a more precise measure of statistical significance. It is simply calculated as the proportion of times the permutation test produced a difference equal or more extreme than the observed difference. Values of 0.05 and 0.10 are commonly used. Of course, the level chosen is somewhat arbitrary.

# calculate p-value

p-value = 0.1134

A p-value of 0.12 means that we would expect to achieve a result as extreme or more extreme by random chance 12% of the time. This suggests that the observed difference in churn rate between the control and treatment group is within the range of chance variation. Essentially, we should be cautious accepting this c. 5% reduction at face value.

From a business perspective this doesn't really answer the more fundemental question: did the loyalty reward reduce churn?

So, rather than simply accept the null hypothesis, chalk the reduction down to random chance and leave it at that, let's extend our analysis by taking a closer look at the difference in churn rates across the two groups.

Bootstrap Resampling - How reliable is our estimate?¶

Bootstrapping involves repeatedly re-sampling the dataset with replacement and calculating the churn rate reduction across these samples. In effect, this is simulating the drawing of multiple samples by using just one sample from a population. The variation in churn rate reduction will give us an indication of how uncertain the numbers are and enable us to calculate confidence intervals for our results and answer other key questions.

In [16]:
# Create a list with bootstrapped means for each group
boot = []
for i in range(10000):
    boot_mean = churn.sample(frac=1, replace=True).groupby('Reward')['Churn Value'].mean()
    boot.append(boot_mean)
    
# Transform list to a DataFrame
boot = pd.DataFrame(boot)

# define an AB test function to return mean differences as a list
def segmentbs(df, filter, samples):
    results = df[df['tenure'] == filter]
    boot = []
    for i in range(samples):
      boot_mean = results.sample(frac=1, replace=True).groupby('Reward')['Churn Value'].mean()
      boot.append(boot_mean)
    boot = pd.DataFrame(boot)
    boot['diff'] = boot['Reward'] - boot['None']
    return boot['diff'].tolist()
